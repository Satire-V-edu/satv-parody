It’s late Sunday, and most people who had a choice chose to fly earlier in the day. Outside, the runways have gotten all nostalgic with the sunset.There’s a delay, but I don’t know what it’s for. I keep following the pattern in the carpet: Xs on a green base, the green lacking just enough blue to miss turquoise. I’ve been here a lot. These Xs are stuck in me: I can’t imagine an airport without them now.I’m trying to convince myself of something.I said goodbye to my mother an hour ago, my sisters an hour before that. I was only here for a little while. I’m going back to work tomorrow in San Francisco. A few weeks left before school.The Xs are mesmerizing.Something isn’t right.

Image courtesy of metmuseum.orgHesiod’s Theogony starts off by telling where the story itself comes from: the muses teach Hesiod a song while he shepherds his lambs. His poems are created by the divine. They’re passed on by the voice of a goddess as it takes over his lungs.The Greek epics open with an invocation. Tell me, muse, of the ingenious hero. Sing, muse, the wrath of Achilles, son of Peleus. Is it a plea or a command? But, either way, it says that inspiration comes from something else.It comes through you, and your job is to get out of the way. You don’t lead it. You follow. So say the Greeks.♠I am eight and out on the wet dock.  It is new summer and my bathing suit is clingy since I have been swimming, but I am too young to mind.
