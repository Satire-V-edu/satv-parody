REVIEW: A Night with “Carly Fiorina Fan Club”

2% milk drips from the ceiling. A half-man, half-horse in a Supreme t-shirt does the dougie on shredded up versions of Derrida’s Of Grammatology, exposing how the black body is liminal and deconstructive but also performative. Three television screens replay a America’s Funniest Home Videos clip of a rotund man falling out of his trampoline. A trenchant critique of late capitalism, or a hackneyed recapitulation of bourgeois norms? I wonder. Everyone in the room is white (except me; I’m Polish.)

 

This is just your average night with “Carly Fiorina Fan Club.”

 

A disgruntled math rock band from the grungy, up-and-coming New York neighborhood of Bushwick, “Carly Fiorina Fan Club” is comprised of five Vassar grads who met while waiting in line for the bathroom at an abandoned pencil store and immediately bonded over their hatred of the Department of Interior. I first started listening to them when my boyfriend Keyboard, a jazz musician, and I accidentally walked into their live show at a twine factory. They were playing their hit track, “Land is theft,” a playful expose on the Department of Interior’s mishandling of Native American affairs. I couldn’t believe my ears. How could they so innovatively resisting the capitalist culture industry?

 

This time their concert was at an Arby’s in North Virginia, next to a gas station and a large suburban megamall. I got there just as their first opener, “Mind Body Juulism,” finished performing a song they wrote only with Gmail smart reply. “Sounds good to me!” they wailed over and over, as three feminists played a very large recorder. All of their names were Brenda.

 

Finally, it was time for “Carly Fiorina Fan Club” to play. The crowd of three middle school students from Bethesda were high energy as the lead singer, Rab, howled through all of the crowd hits: “Meat Mountain,” a haunting R&B Soundcloud ballad about secret menu items at American fast food chains, and “Golden State Killer,” a sparkly, synth-filled misandrist bop about the lack of female serial killers in the killing industry.

 

On the drive home I stuck my head out of my great aunt’s Hyundai Elantra and let the wind blow through my hair, contemplating my mortality and Jeff Goldblum. It was the best night ever.
