Rumor: Good Kid, M.A.A.D Cité: Georgio in Twenty-First-Century Paris

Liza Batkin ’15

It’s of some interest to watch new music videos released in 2015 by the French rapper Booba if only to be sobered by their backwardness. The Rolls Royce-driving, Yankees-cap-wearing, 2000s-rap-troping Booba—a cultural annex of the US and of questionable originality—is arguably the most recognizable name in current French hip-hop. Sometimes he raps about how other rappers have fewer twitter followers than he. And he garners upwards of 2 million views on every stacks-and-sportscar-intensive video he puts out.

Somewhere on the opposite end of Francophone rap’s aesthetic spectrum are what we might call hoards of young, unaffiliated, often faceless rappers whose medium of choice is the pithy freestyle. Their music videos are largely in the throwback style of Joey Bada$$—the crew riding through their neighborhood and exerting some kind of imagined dominance over their surroundings. Fragmented into innumerable collectives and creative partnerships, their presence depends almost entirely on the hype they can inspire.

Georgio, 22 years old and a native of Paris’s 18th arrondissement, belongs to this second category. He’s been inspiring hype since he put out his 2013 EP Soleil d’hiver and has a devoted, vocal following. He’s not signed, no Wikipedia article exists on him, his most popular videos haven’t broken 1,000,000 views, and apparently he doesn’t even make enough money to avoid having to steal food from Monoprix (Monoprix is like a French CVS, except much cuter and more varied in its inventory).

He doesn’t have a gimmick, either. Lacking in the narrative virtuosity of, say, Kendrick Lamar or the meta-rap discourse of Earl Sweatshirt, Georgio relies on little more than engaging wordplay, a personal rapport with his fans, and the appearance of keeping it real. Taking rap back to the ‘90s realism of Kool G Rap and early Jay Z, Georgio’s oeuvre is largely a portrait of Parisian street life for a marginalized class of adolescents, delivered with an attitude somewhere between indignant and resigned. “Dans le quartier c’est pas les hommes mais c’est les balles qui percent,” (in this neighborhood, it’s not people that break through but bullets) he raps on “Tu sais c’qui s’passe” in a slick play on the double meaning of percer—to pierce, or to break through into mainstream success.

Or consider his menacingly good verse on “A tous les coups,” in which he brandishes his youth (“I started my revolution before I even knew how to boil pasta”), questions the status quo (“Why did 3/4 of my bros grow up without a dad?”), and links the motif of this track to the popular expression faire les quatre cents coups (“to raise hell”), a motif shared by François Truffaut’s seminal Nouvelle Vague film, Les 400 coups (The 400 Blows). Whether or not Georgio intended to remind us of it, Truffaut’s film famously centers on a troublemaking adolescent growing up in Paris—not unlike Georgio’s portrait of himself as a lawbreaking “p’tit con” (little asshole) in the first line of “Conclusion.”

Yet, often reluctant to rap about defying the law without acknowledging its personal effects, Georgio begins the title track of his 2014 EP A l’abri by admitting that “Our errors are what hurt our mothers the worst… it wasn’t easy for us, with just as much love as screams.” This willingness to address personal or social problems reappears in “Le poing levé les yeux bandés,” also off l’abri, in which he reminds us that “we grew up like wolves, not those bougie kids who take classes at the Sorbonne” while complicating the “live fast, die young” rap trope: “I’ve placed in question the value of a long life; I’m like Nietzsche, they’ll only understand me once I die.” Flattering comparisons will always exaggerate, but I think that it’s lines like this, in which an American rap fan might hear the echo of Mos Def’s deft lyricism or Nas’s Illmatic-era erudition, that make Georgio worth listening to.

But my focus on Georgio’s precocious gravity doesn’t mean he lacks a sense of humor. In his ‘80s-kitsch video for “Blackjack,” he raps about crack destroying families over a moving background of absurd casino-themed stock footage while wearing a Steve-Jobs style black turtleneck and a lipstick-red blazer. This chroma keying gets pushed to its natural, absurd extremes. At one point in the video, a salt-shaker-sized Georgio stands pan-faced, rapping on the green felt of a blackjack table while surrounded by relatively enormous betting chips.

Nevertheless, Georgio’s Lacoste feels dated (even though he’s explained in an interview that this is intentional), his instrumentals are far from pushing genre barriers, and as I mentioned earlier, there are many, many independent rappers in France—in Paris—engaged in a similar stylistic project (the potentially-offensive Vald¹ and the stone-cold members of TSR CREW² to name only a few). But what’s exciting about Georgio is his return to locality and the feeling of authenticity it engenders in an era of increasingly cosmopolitan rap. As he puts it in “Le poing levé,” “I’ve got the choice between my neighborhood or touring the world.” So far, he’s chosen his neighborhood. So when he shouts “Putain d’XV3” (fucking XV3 = the 18th arrondissement) or “MARX DO” (short for Marx Dormoy, the metro-stop classification for Georgio’s neighborhood) before a particularly hard-hitting verse, you get the feeling that this is more than mindless self-branding. Factor in his surgical flow and ingenious writing, and Georgio becomes just as catchily synecdochic as these nicknames for his neighborhood—he stands in for a whole clan of listeners, friends, and p’tits cons. As he reminds us in “Tu sais c’qui s’passe,” “Just like you, I’m from the poor families of France—even if there are worse.”

Yet the question of whom exactly he represents when he makes these comments is impossible to avoid. Of Caribbean origin, Georgio is part of the postcolonial legacy of immigrants in France.³ Facing economic adversity, he belongs to a certain social milieu. (He’s not a boogie kid who takes courses at the Sorbonne, remember?) And in most of his music videos, northern Paris is the default scenery, backing up Georgio’s affirmations of local identity. In the video for his newest track “Indomptable,” he raps in what seem to be dimly-lit Parisian alcoves while dropping lines like “I always speak the truth, independent like Gandhi… It’s 1 for my province, then 2 for my Parisians.” So while Georgio’s incontrovertibly French, there’s something new about his Frenchness, something that makes him inseparable from conversations about social and ethnic divisions in twenty-first-century France. If you’re feeling generous, you might think of Balzac’s iconic Eugène de Rastignac, the young man disillusioned by Parisian society who looks out over the city from the north and says, “Henceforth there is war between us.” But if you believe, like David Brooks, that in French hip-hop there is no progress, only blind appropriation, then you might consider Georgio a simple successor to OG hip-hop acts of the ‘90s like Suprême NTM and Ärsenik—that is, part of a new generation of cultural kidney stones France needs to pass.

In the meantime, Georgio’s fan base is growing and his flow is getting tighter. His writing has matured, linking countless references and quips together and proving his technical excellence again and again. So maybe technical excellence and street cred. are all Georgio needs to stay relevant. Maybe they’re not. We’ll see. As Jean Cocteau has noted, “Paris has an ostrich’s stomach. It digests everything. It assimilates nothing.”

¹ In this video Vald plays the role of a young man who murders his family while rapping “I fuck the world as if I were autistic.”

² Hugo has recently cited Hugo Boss of TSR CREW as one of his influences.

³  In “Homme de l’ombre,” Georgio says both that he’s “from Guadeloupe” and that he’s “proud to come from Pointe à Pitre,” Guadeloupe’s primary urban center.
