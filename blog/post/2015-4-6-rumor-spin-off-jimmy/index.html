Rumor: Spin-off Jimmy

Maia Silber ’17

Tired-looking women in sleek ponytails and dirty polo shirts wipe a speckled counter, elbows rigid and rags dry. The camera focuses on bright frosting in swirls—up close, constellation-like—Cinnabons spinning on silver trays. In the background, 1940’s-style swing music plays.

The whole scene runs in black-and-white, but it’s not a flashback. It’s a flash-forward, a glimpse of Saul’s fall and a reference to the Breaking Bad quip: “If I’m lucky, best case scenario, I’m managing a Cinnabon in Omaha.”

The scene targeted the pulsing pleasure-center of every television addict’s brain. For some critics wary of the spin-off tendency to thrive on crass, easy references, it raised eyebrows and lowered pens. Since we already know the fate of Saul (nee Jimmy McGill)—how the Cinnabon crumbles, so to speak—the prequel could only provide “cheap hits of fanservice.”

“I want to see what happens to Jimmy McGill,” wrote Eric Thurm in an otherwise positive review for The Verge. “I just wish I didn’t already know.”

Yet, six episodes later, we’re still watching. That’s partly because the fanservice appeals to parts of the brain outside the pleasure-center. Characters plucked from Breaking Bad, like the fixer Mike Armantrout and the drug lord Tuco, gain new depth on the series. The Cinnabon joke might have been a cheap hit, but Mike’s tragic backstory is Walter White grade crystal—almost 100% pure.

It’s also because the show’s not really about what happens to Jimmy McGill. Cinnabon-serving aside, he has only so many options, anyway. He can succeed at the cost of his soul, or he can fail at the cost of his sanity. Howard Hamlin, the pastel-wearing powerhouse who speaks in a vernacular of sarcastic clichés—“Sometimes in our line of work you forget to listen to your heart”—represents the first option. Chuck McGill, Jimmy’s mentally ill brother and Hamlin’s one-time partner, who hides under a space blanket when confronted with electromagnetism or corruption, represents the second.

In Breaking Bad, Peter Gould gave us the rise-and-fall, the Shakespearean arc, the tragic hero. He also gave us the self-made man: Walter White-cum-Heisenberg, like Jimmy Gatz-cum-Jay Gatsby, lived the American Dream of self-invention, suave headwear and all. Like most American Dream narratives, it ended in a nightmare. The self-made man self-destructed.

Jimmy McGill, though, cannot really rise and fall, because he’s not a self-made man: he’s a spin-off. In Episode Three, “Hero,” Hamlin takes Jimmy to court for copying the Hamlin & McGill logo—and even Hamlin’s outfit– on a billboard. Earlier, we watch Jimmy walk into a store, bypassing the orange dress shirts he wears on Breaking Bad for Hamlin’s signature blue (“Hamlindigo”). Hamlin easily convinces the judge to charge Jimmy with copyright infringement, but he can’t stop Jimmy from using his name on the billboard.

It’s a meta scene—a subtle jab at critics who initially charged the show with copyright infringement. As we cheer on this Robin-Hood-esque theft, we celebrate the spin-off, not the genius of self-invention but the cleverness of reinvention.

Jimmy, like Better Call Saul itself, molds himself with the words and images of others. In a conference-room argument with Hamlin, Jimmy shouts, hands up, “You will atone!” quoting The Network. After convincing a corrupt local politician to pay him a “retainer” of dirty money, Saul mutters, “Upon this rock, I will build my church.” Jimmy’s no Puritan; he’s a conman, and his best con is the copy-con.

Cheap hits of fanservice? Preparing to specialize in elder care law, Jimmy watches Matlock, legal pad in hand; the next day, he arrives at a senior home in Andy Griffith’s signature white suit. Better Call Saul does not only spin off of Breaking Bad, it spins off of the entire television canon. Jimmy succeeds because he has mastered the art of reference; instead of inventing himself, he tinkers around with others’ inventions.

Watching Breaking Bad, white-knuckled viewers asked, “can he do it”? Can Walt use an electromagnet to rob a police safe? Can he siphon off chemicals from a train? Can he rig an old man’s wheelchair with a bomb? As the show went on, “can he do it”? became “would he do it?” Would he let a woman die? Would he poison a child? Would he kill without remorse?

On Better Call Saul, the stakes often seem lower, the schemes a touch less theatrical. Jimmy does have a few tricks up his Hamlindigo blue sleeve—in one scene, he tricks a woman into revealing her cash-stash by planting a separate pile of money, sprayed with fluorescent liquid, in her living room. Yet, his successes tend to be tempered: he gives the woman up to a rival law firm. And he never has to kill. So, we don’t ask “can he?” or “would he?” We just ask, “how”?

When a con-gone-wrong lands Jimmy and two teenagers in the hands of a budding drug lord, there’s no dramatic rescue scene. But Jimmy convinces the meth-dealer to break the teenagers’ legs instead of killing them. When the kids complain, Jimmy objects, “I just talked you down from a death sentence to six months probation—I’m the best lawyer ever!” Later, Jimmy talks down another dealer: “I just want to open a dialogue.” While Breaking Bad, dealt in action, physical and moral, Better Call Saul deals in talk. It matters less what Jimmy does than how he presents it. That’s why the particular arc of his rise and fall—cons to cinnabons—does not matter. Whether he succeeds or fails at any given moment, it’ll just open a dialogue.

The idea for Better Call Saul came out of a writer’s room joke. When the creators of Breaking Bad thought of a line or a scene too silly for the epic drama, they would say “save it for the Saul Goodman show.” If Breaking Bad—whose 2008 premier coincided with the start of the Great Recession—appealed to audiences discovering that the American Dream had failed, Better Call Saul appeals to audiences who can narrate its collapse in their dreams. We’ve seen the ending—the Cinnabons at the end of the tunnel—and we can only hope the climb (or the fall) will be interesting.

We do have two American Dreamers on the show—Jimmy’s naïve clients. One plasters his walls with mountain lion and zebra heads, and asks Jimmy to help him secede from the United States in order to escape “the fly-swatting government.” In what might be the show’s funniest scene, a man invents a weirdly sexual talking toilet, and thinks he’ll make it big. These characters are absurdist parodies of Walter White, “inventors” seeking repute, money, and independence.

Jimmy knows better. Standing in front of his copied billboard, he looks straight at the camera (he’s hired the crew of a college news station). “Like most Americans, I’m a self-made man,” Jimmy says slyly. “I’ve also had a dream, a dream that many of us have had, to own my business.”

Jimmy’s a spin-off and the billboard’s a rip-off, but the joke’s on us. These are the best cheap hits around.
